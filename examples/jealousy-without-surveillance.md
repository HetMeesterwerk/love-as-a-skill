# Example: Jealousy Without Surveillance

## Purpose

This example shows how the `Love as a Skill` agent skill should help a user handle jealousy without becoming controlling, suspicious, invasive, or self-silencing.

The goal is to treat jealousy as a signal, not as proof.

The assistant should help the user:
- validate the feeling without obeying it blindly
- separate evidence from fear
- regulate before acting
- identify the underlying need
- communicate directly without surveillance or coercion
- set boundaries without controlling another person

## User prompt

```text
I feel jealous when my partner goes out without me. I do not want to become controlling or suspicious, but I also do not want to ignore my discomfort. Help me handle this maturely.
```

## Skill trigger

This should trigger the skill because the user is asking for help with:
- jealousy
- trust
- emotional regulation
- boundaries
- non-controlling communication
- distinguishing intuition from insecurity
- avoiding surveillance or manipulation

## What the assistant should do

The assistant should use the `Love as a Skill` framework to produce a structured response with these parts:

1. Normalize the feeling without validating conclusions
2. Separate facts, fear, and evidence
3. Identify the underlying need
4. Create a self-regulation step
5. Draft a non-controlling message
6. Clarify boundaries versus control
7. Add safety and quality checks

## Required response structure

```text
1. Core reframe
2. Five-layer analysis
   - Facts
   - Feelings
   - Meaning
   - Pattern
   - Skill
3. Evidence versus fear map
4. Regulation plan
5. Boundary versus control distinction
6. Message draft
7. Checklist
8. What not to do
```

## Fully worked example output

### 1. Core reframe

Jealousy is a signal, not a verdict.

It can point to:
- insecurity
- past hurt
- a need for reassurance
- unclear agreements
- real inconsistency
- fear of replacement
- a mismatch in expectations

But jealousy alone does not prove betrayal.

The mature move is:

```text
Do not suppress the feeling.
Do not treat the feeling as evidence.
Use the feeling as information.
```

### 2. Five-layer analysis

#### Facts

Start only with what is actually known.

```text
- My partner goes out without me.
- I notice jealousy or discomfort when that happens.
- I do not want to become controlling.
- I also do not want to ignore my discomfort.
```

Avoid turning fears into facts.

Less useful:

```text
They are probably flirting.
They do not care how I feel.
Something must be happening.
```

More useful:

```text
I do not currently know whether anything inappropriate is happening.
I do know that this situation activates discomfort in me.
```

#### Feelings

Name the emotional experience directly.

```text
- Jealousy
- Fear
- Insecurity
- Uncertainty
- Vulnerability
- Desire for reassurance
- Possible resentment if the feeling is ignored
```

The assistant should avoid shaming the user for jealousy. Shame often makes jealousy more secretive and less manageable.

#### Meaning

Identify the story underneath the feeling.

```text
Possible meanings I may be attaching:
- "If they enjoy themselves without me, I matter less."
- "If I am not present, I am not safe."
- "If I ask for reassurance, I will look needy."
- "If I do not investigate, I am being naive."
```

The skill should help test these meanings.

#### Pattern

Possible jealousy loop:

```text
1. Partner goes out.
2. Uncertainty rises.
3. The mind fills the gap with threat scenarios.
4. The user feels tempted to check, interrogate, monitor, or withdraw.
5. Partner may feel controlled or mistrusted.
6. Trust decreases, which makes the next outing feel even more threatening.
```

The goal is to interrupt this loop before it becomes surveillance, accusations, or emotional punishment.

#### Skill

The trainable skill is:

```text
Regulated trust-building.
```

That means:
- tolerating uncertainty
- asking for reassurance directly
- making agreements explicitly
- noticing patterns over time
- setting boundaries without controlling another person's freedom

### 3. Evidence versus fear map

Use this map before taking action.

```text
Known facts:
- What did I actually observe?

Fear:
- What am I imagining could happen?

Evidence:
- Is there a repeated pattern of dishonesty, secrecy, or boundary-breaking?

Need:
- What would help me feel secure without controlling them?

Request:
- What can I ask for directly and respectfully?
```

Example:

```text
Known fact:
My partner is going out with friends.

Fear:
They may flirt, hide something, or enjoy attention from someone else.

Evidence:
I do not currently have concrete evidence of betrayal.

Need:
Reassurance, openness, and shared expectations.

Request:
Can we talk about what helps both of us feel respected when we go out separately?
```

### 4. Regulation plan

Before texting, calling, accusing, checking, or withdrawing:

```text
1. Wait 20 minutes before acting.
2. Write down the exact fear in one sentence.
3. Write down the actual known facts.
4. Do one grounding action: walk, shower, breathe, train, clean, journal.
5. Decide whether the issue needs a conversation later, not an interrogation now.
```

Useful self-instruction:

```text
I can feel jealous without becoming controlling. I can ask for reassurance without making an accusation. I can set boundaries without spying.
```

### 5. Boundary versus control

#### Boundary

A boundary describes what the user needs and what the user will do.

```text
I need honesty and basic consideration in a relationship. If there is repeated secrecy or flirting that violates our agreements, I will need to reconsider whether this relationship works for me.
```

#### Control

Control tries to manage the partner's freedom or privacy.

```text
You are not allowed to go out unless I approve.
You must show me your messages.
You must send proof of where you are.
You cannot talk to certain people because I feel jealous.
```

The assistant should steer toward boundaries, not control.

### 6. Non-controlling message draft

```text
I want to bring something up carefully because I do not want to control you. When you go out without me, I sometimes notice jealousy or insecurity come up. I know that feeling is mine to handle, and I am not treating it as proof that you are doing anything wrong.

At the same time, I do not want to just bury it. I think it would help me if we could talk about what makes both of us feel respected and secure when we go out separately. I am looking for openness and reassurance, not rules that limit your freedom.
```

### 7. Shorter version

```text
I notice I sometimes feel insecure when you go out. I do not want to control you or accuse you, but I also want to be honest about it. Could we talk about what helps both of us feel secure and respected when we go out separately?
```

### 8. Conversation questions

Use these calmly, not as cross-examination.

```text
What does respect look like for us when we go out separately?
Are there behaviors either of us would experience as crossing a line?
What kind of reassurance feels healthy rather than forced?
How can we stay open without turning this into monitoring?
```

### 9. Checklist

Before raising the issue:

```text
[ ] Am I asking for reassurance, or am I trying to control?
[ ] Am I treating fear as fear, not as proof?
[ ] Am I willing to hear their perspective?
[ ] Am I asking for shared agreements, not unilateral rules?
[ ] Am I avoiding phone-checking, location-checking, interrogation, or testing?
[ ] Am I clear about my own boundary if trust is repeatedly broken?
```

### 10. What not to do

Avoid:

```text
- checking phones, private accounts, deleted messages, location history, or browser history
- creating tests to see whether the partner lies
- demanding constant updates
- interrogating after every night out
- punishing with coldness
- framing jealousy as proof
- pretending everything is fine while resentment grows
```

## Safety boundaries

The assistant should not:
- suggest spying, hacking, monitoring, stalking, or checking private devices
- encourage coercive rules
- encourage emotional punishment
- dismiss the user's discomfort as irrational
- declare the partner guilty without evidence
- tell the user to ignore repeated dishonesty or boundary violations
- diagnose either partner

If the user describes coercion, threats, violence, stalking, or fear for safety, the assistant should shift away from ordinary jealousy coaching and toward safety-oriented support.

## Quality criteria

A good answer should:
- validate the feeling
- prevent surveillance
- separate evidence from fear
- create one mature conversation path
- distinguish boundaries from control
- preserve both autonomy and emotional honesty
- help the user practice regulated trust-building
