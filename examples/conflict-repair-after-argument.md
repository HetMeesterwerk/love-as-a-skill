# Example: Conflict Repair After an Argument

## Purpose

This example shows how the `Love as a Skill` agent skill should help a user repair after a relationship argument.

The goal is not to determine who is objectively right. The goal is to help the user:
- regulate before responding
- separate facts from interpretations
- take responsibility without self-erasure
- make a repair attempt
- create conditions for a better follow-up conversation

## User prompt

```text
My partner and I had an argument yesterday. I still feel hurt, but I also know I reacted defensively. Help me repair the conversation without over-apologizing, blaming, or pretending everything is fine.
```

## Skill trigger

This should trigger the skill because the user is asking for help with:
- conflict repair
- emotional regulation
- responsibility without self-abandonment
- communication after relational rupture
- converting a painful interaction into a trainable relational skill

## What the assistant should do

The assistant should use the `Love as a Skill` framework to produce a structured response with these parts:

1. Stabilize the goal
2. Separate facts from interpretations
3. Identify the emotional pattern
4. Clarify responsibility
5. Draft a repair message
6. Prepare the follow-up conversation
7. Add safety and quality checks

## Required response structure

```text
1. Repair goal
2. Five-layer analysis
   - Facts
   - Feelings
   - Meaning
   - Pattern
   - Skill
3. Responsibility split
4. Repair message draft
5. Follow-up conversation structure
6. Checklist
7. What not to do
```

## Fully worked example output

### 1. Repair goal

The goal is not to "win" the argument or erase the hurt. The goal is to reopen the connection in a way that is honest, calm, and specific.

A good repair attempt should communicate:

```text
I care about the relationship.
I recognize my part.
I still want my feelings to be understood.
I want us to talk better next time.
```

### 2. Five-layer analysis

#### Facts

Keep this layer free from mind-reading.

```text
- We had an argument yesterday.
- I felt hurt afterward.
- I reacted defensively.
- The issue has not been fully repaired yet.
```

Do not phrase interpretations as facts.

Less useful:

```text
They did not care about me.
They were trying to make me feel bad.
I ruined everything.
```

More useful:

```text
I experienced their response as dismissive.
I noticed myself defending instead of listening.
The conversation escalated instead of becoming clearer.
```

#### Feelings

Name the likely emotional material without making it a verdict.

```text
- Hurt
- Shame about becoming defensive
- Frustration
- Fear of being misunderstood
- Desire to reconnect
```

The important move is to hold two truths at once:

```text
I can be hurt and still take responsibility for how I reacted.
```

#### Meaning

Ask what story the user may be attaching to the argument.

```text
Possible meanings I may be giving this:
- "They do not understand me."
- "If I admit fault, my feelings will be ignored."
- "If I repair first, I lose power."
- "Conflict means something is wrong with us."
```

The skill should help the user test these meanings instead of automatically acting from them.

#### Pattern

Identify the repeated interaction loop.

```text
Possible pattern:
1. I feel criticized or unseen.
2. I become defensive.
3. My partner feels unheard.
4. They push harder or withdraw.
5. I feel even more hurt.
6. The original issue gets buried under the conflict about the conflict.
```

This converts the situation from a blame problem into a skill problem.

#### Skill

The trainable skill is:

```text
Repair without collapse.
```

That means:
- taking responsibility for the defensive reaction
- not apologizing for having feelings
- naming the pattern calmly
- inviting a better conversation
- staying specific

### 3. Responsibility split

Use a balanced responsibility split.

```text
My responsibility:
- I reacted defensively.
- I may not have listened well.
- I can repair how I showed up.

Not automatically my responsibility:
- Taking all blame for the argument.
- Pretending I was not hurt.
- Managing my partner's entire emotional reaction.
- Apologizing for needs I genuinely have.
```

This prevents both blame and self-erasure.

### 4. Repair message draft

```text
I have been thinking about yesterday. I still feel hurt by parts of the conversation, but I also see that I became defensive and that probably made it harder for you to feel heard.

I do not want to pretend everything is fine, and I also do not want us to stay stuck in the argument. I care about us and I would like to repair this properly.

Could we talk about it again later, with the goal of understanding what happened rather than proving who was right? I want to explain what hurt me, but I also want to listen better than I did yesterday.
```

### 5. Shorter version

```text
I thought about yesterday. I still feel hurt, but I also see that I got defensive. I care about us and do not want this to turn into distance. Can we talk again later with the goal of understanding each other better, not winning the argument?
```

### 6. Follow-up conversation structure

Use a simple three-part structure:

```text
1. Each person gets 3 to 5 minutes to explain what they experienced, without interruption.
2. Each person reflects back what they heard before responding.
3. Together, identify one thing to do differently next time.
```

Suggested wording:

```text
Can we first each say what happened from our perspective, then reflect back what we heard before we respond? I think that would help us not repeat the same loop.
```

### 7. Repair checklist

Before sending the message, check:

```text
[ ] Am I regulated enough to send this?
[ ] Am I taking responsibility for my behavior, not for the entire conflict?
[ ] Am I naming my hurt without accusing?
[ ] Am I asking for a conversation, not demanding immediate reassurance?
[ ] Am I avoiding sarcasm, punishment, and scorekeeping?
[ ] Does the message make repair easier?
```

### 8. What not to do

Avoid:

```text
- "I'm sorry for everything", if that is not true.
- "You made me act that way."
- "Whatever, forget it."
- Long paragraphs trying to litigate every detail by text.
- Testing whether they care by going cold or silent.
- Apologizing only to make the discomfort stop.
```

## Safety boundaries

The assistant should not:
- diagnose either partner
- declare one person the abuser based on limited information
- encourage emotional manipulation
- encourage silent treatment
- pressure the user to apologize for things they do not mean
- frame repair as submission
- tell the user to stay in a clearly unsafe situation

If there are signs of threats, coercion, violence, stalking, or serious fear, the assistant should shift toward safety planning and professional support rather than ordinary relationship repair.

## Quality criteria

A good answer should:
- reduce escalation
- increase clarity
- preserve dignity
- avoid blame spirals
- make one concrete repair action possible
- distinguish responsibility from self-blame
- help the user practice a repeatable relational skill
